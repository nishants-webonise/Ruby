							<div itemprop="description">
<p>The weather has finally decided to cool down around here and the first acorn squash have appeared at our local farmers market. Acorn squash are wonderfully easy to prepare – just cut them in half, scoop out the seeds, add a little brown sugar and/or maple syrup, and bake for a little over an hour. From Google Definitions,</p>
<blockquote><p>Choose squash that are heavy for their size and have a hard, deep-colored rind free of blemishes or moldy spots. The hard skin of a winter squash protects the flesh and allows it to be stored longer than summer squash. It does not require refrigeration and can be kept in a cool, dark place for a month or more, depending on the variety. Once the seeds are removed, winter squash can be baked, steamed or simmered. They’re a good source of iron, riboflavin and vitamins A (more than summer squash) and C.</p></blockquote>
</div>

									<h2>Classic Baked Acorn Squash Recipe</h2>
									
																			<div class="recipe-meta">
											<ul>
<li class="recipe-prep">
<span class="recipemeta-label">Prep time:</span> <span class="preptime" itemprop="prepTime" content="PT00H10M">10 minutes<span class="value-title" title="PT00H10M"></span></span>
</li>
<li class="recipe-cook">
<span class="recipemeta-label">Cook time:</span> <span class="cooktime" itemprop="cookTime" content="PT01H15M">1 hour, 15 minutes<span class="value-title" title="PT01H15M"></span></span>
</li>											
											</ul>
</div>

											<h3>Ingredients</h3>
											<ul>
<li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">1 Acorn squash</li>
<li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">1 Tbsp Butter</li>
<li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">2 Tbsp Brown Sugar</li>
<li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">2 teaspoons Maple Syrup</li>
<li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">Dash of Salt</li>
</ul>
</div>
																	
									<div id="recipe-method" class="entry-details instructions">
										<h3>Method</h3>
										<div itemprop="recipeInstructions">
<p><strong>1</strong> Preheat oven to 400°F.</p>
<p><strong>2</strong> Using a strong chef's knife, and perhaps a rubber mallet to help, cut the acorn squash in half, lengthwise, from stem to end. Use a spoon to scoop out the seeds and stringy stuff in the center of each half. Score the insides of each half several times with a sharp knife. Place each half in a baking pan, cut side up. Add about a 1/4 inch of water to the bottom of the baking pan so that the skins don't burn and the squash doesn't get dried out.</p>
<p><strong>3</strong> Coat the inside of each half with 1/2 a Tbsp of butter. Add a dash of salt if you are using unsalted butter. Add a Tbsp of brown sugar to the cavity of each half. Dribble on a teaspoon of maple syrup to each half.</p>
<p><strong>4</strong> Bake in the oven for 1 hour to 1 hour 15 minutes, until the squash is very soft and the tops are browned. Do not undercook. When finished, remove from oven and let cool a little before serving. Spoon any buttery sugar sauce that has not already been absorbed by the squash over the exposed areas.</p>
</div>
										<p><strong>Yield:</strong> <span class="yield" itemprop="recipeYield">Serves 2 to 4, depending on how much squash you like to eat.</span></p>									</div>

<p>Tks everyone for tips. Here are 2 more:<br>
1. Squash becomes easy schmeazy to cut if you bake it first.<br>
2. Two things to do with seeds: (A) If you’re baking the squash, you can leave them there til the last 15 min, then tease them to the top with a fork so you can butter/spice them up for a crunchy topping. (B) If you just like roasted seeds, leave them in the squash while you bake. Easiest way to remove them for roasting is to take 2 forks and lift them out. Fork tines at an angle to each other make good shears to lop off the strings. I save seeds in the freezer til I have lots to roast.</p>
